Results:

Textual analysis of the publications of The Georgia Historical Quarterly tells us a lot about why and how the Georgia Historical Society chose to consider Native Americans important enough to Georgia's history to include them in the quarterly. Although the quarterly was created by scholars, its representation of Native Americans can reveal a lot about Georgians' historical memory of Native Americans.

This visualization showcases the total number of words relating to Native Americans that appeared in each year of The Georgia Historical Quarterly's publications. There are three notable things that this visualization reveals. The first of which is that the quarterly's references to Native Americans have increased in recent years, demonstrating a relatively consistent change over time. Beginning in 1990 and going until 2015, the most recent year there is data for, mentions of Native Americans have been higher than every other previous year, except for the outliers 1955 and 1970.

The outliers of 1955 and 1970 provide valuable insights into what kinds of events might increase the GHS's likelihood of including Native Americans in the quarterly. In 1968, President Lyndon B. Johnson signed the Indian Civil Rights Act, which granted Native American tribes the benefits included in the Bill of Rights. 1  Considering that the most mentions of Native Americans in the quarterly occurred in 1970, it could be concluded that the GHS was more likely to include Native Americans in the quarterly when they believed Native Americans were politically relevant. Furthermore, the uptick in mentions of Native Americans in the quarterly in 1955 could be attributed to the larger trend of Georgians' wanting to atone for their ancestors' wrongs towards Native Americans, as evidenced by the rebuilding of New Echota shortly after in the 1960s. 2

This visualization showcases the number of times that the word "Cherokee" was used in each year of the GHQ publications. In this visualization, the year 1950 is an obvious outlier in the number of mentions of Cherokees in the GHQ, with Cherokees being referenced 225 times this year as opposed to the next highest number of references, 88. Although a slightly closer reading will reveal that one of the volumes from 1950 included an article solely about the Cherokee Phoenix, which would account for most of the Cherokee mentions, it is still significant that the GHS chose to include an article solely about Cherokees in the GHQ. The timing of this in 1950 could also be demonstrative of the larger trend of Georgians' wanting to atone for their ancestors' wrongs towards Native Americans, as evidenced by the rebuilding of New Echota shortly after in the 1960s. 3

This visualization showcases how often words relating to Cherokee Removal appear within 5 words of the word “Cherokee” by creating different sized bubbles proportioned according to their totals. From this visualization, it does not seem that the GHQ tends only to include Cherokees in the context of removal, considering that the word “Cherokee” only appears in the context of Cherokee Removal about 11% of the time.

This visualization showcases how often words relating to white people appear within 5 words of words relating to Native Americans by creating different sized bubbles proportioned according to their totals. As seen in the visualization, almost 17% of the times that Native Americans are referenced, they are referenced in relation to white people. This is still a relatively small portion of the overall words relating to Native Americans in the GHQ, but it is still significant that in 80 different volumes of the GHQ, which all average about 150 pages (totaling about 12,000 pages), words relating to Native Americans only appear without being connected to white people 1,364 times.

This visualization showcases what percentage of words relating to white people appear in the sets of the top 3 words that are within 5 words of words referencing Native Americans. Although words referencing Native Americans might have only appeared in context with words relating to white people about 17% of the time, about 61% of the sets of the top 3 words that are within 5 words of words referencing Native Americans contained a word relating to white people.

This visualization showcases the distribution of what order words relating to white people appeared in the top 3 words that are within 5 words of words referencing Native Americans. The most significant aspect of this visualization is that there are 28 total words relating to white people, and only 22 sets of the top 3 words appearing within 5 five words of words referencing Native Americans contained words relating to white people. This demonstrates that in 6 different cases, words relating to white people made up not only 1 but 2 out of the top 3 words that appear within 5 words of words referencing Native Americans.

This visualization showcases what percentage of words relating to Cherokee Removal appear in the sets of the top 3 words that are within 5 words of the word “Cherokee.” Although it is still less than half at about 44%, this visualization showcases that almost half of the years of the GHQ's topics regarding Cherokees still focused largely on Cherokee Removal.

This visualization showcases the distribution of what order words relating to Cherokee Removal appeared in the top 3 words that are within 5 words of the word “Cherokee.” This visualization demonstrates that words relating to Cherokee Removal were in the top 2 words the majority of the time.
