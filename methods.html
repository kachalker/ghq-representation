Methods:

Source Materials:

The primary source for this project was The Georgia Historical Quarterly (GHQ) that has been published from 1917-2018 by the Georgia Historical Society. For this project, all volumes from every five years of the GHQ publications (1920-2015) were run through textual analysis. These volumes were downloaded from JSTOR, and you can view them here.

There were a variety of other secondary sources that were referenced to guide this project including journal articles, book chapters, and websites. You can view a full list of those sources and their roles in my project by viewing my annotated bibliography here.

Data Analysis Techniques:

I began analyzing the data by downloading every volume I needed from JSTOR, and I sorted them into folders by year. Then, I uploaded those folders to the textual analysis software, AntConc, to begin my textual analysis.

I utilized 2 functions within AntConc for my analysis: KWIC and collocate. I used KWIC to find the overall times that words relating to Native Americans (Indian, native, indigenous, tribe, and the names of Georgia's 2 state-recognized tribes, Cherokee and Creek) appeared in each years' volumes of the GHQ. However, because it is impossible for a textual analysis software to tell what a word means, I had to do a slightly closer reading of where these words appeared, such as making sure “creek” related to the Creek tribe and not the body of water. I created Excel sheets with this data that you can view here.

I then used the collocate function to view all words within 5 words to the left or right of a particular word to look at words relating to Native Americans to find what context words relating to Native Americans appeared in. I specifically looked if they appeared with words indicating white people (trade, French, land, goods, colonial, White, boundary, territory, British, Spanish, settlement, European).

Inspired by Andrew Denson's work about Georgians' tendency to view Cherokees only through the lens of Cherokee Removal and connect them to the Lost Cause Myth, I also used the collocate function to look for the appearance of words near the word “Cherokee” that could indicate this (sovereignty, removal, territory, lands, rights, dispossession). 1

Lastly, I looked at the top three words within 5 words left or right of words relating to Native Americans and the word “Cherokee.” I recorded all of this data on Excel sheets. While recording the overall count of the words appearing near words relating to Native American and the word “Cherokee,” I combined any words that meant the same in context (e.g. trade and trader), and I only recorded the top 3 words of years that that had 3 words appearing at least 3 times and I excluded any names of authors of the GHQ and filler words (the, and, a, etc.). Also, because AntConc requires you to search singular and plural forms of a word, I created data sets for both singular and plural forms of the words I was examining. I also highlighted all lines that have data available in yellow and all words relating to white people or removal in green.
